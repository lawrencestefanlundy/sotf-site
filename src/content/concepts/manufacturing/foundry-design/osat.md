# OSAT (Outsourced Semi Assembly + Test)

*Kind: technology*

## Physics / mechanism

OSAT covers the back-end-of-line (BEOL) operations after wafer fabrication: die singulation, wire-bond or flip-chip attach, underfill, moulding, and electrical/functional test. Key players—ASE, Amkor, JCET, Powertech—operate at scale (ASE alone turns ~$17B revenue). Performance parameters that matter: bump pitch (down to 40 µm for advanced flip-chip), co-planarity tolerances (<15 µm for automotive-grade), test coverage (>99.5% for ISO 26262), and thermal resistance of the package stack. Advanced OSAT now includes 2.5D interposer integration, fan-out wafer-level packaging (FOWLP), and chiplet assembly—blurring the line with foundry logic.

## Competitive landscape

ASE/Amkor duopoly dominates advanced nodes; JCET and Powertech hold cost-sensitive segments. Direct competition comes from IDMs internalising back-end (Intel Foundry, Samsung). Adjacent: substrate suppliers (Ibiden, Shinko) often capture more margin than assemblers. Heterogeneous integration shifts value toward foundry-side advanced packaging (TSMC CoWoS, Intel EMIB), threatening pure-play OSATs on the leading edge. Mature-node packaging remains sticky—commodity wire-bond for sensors and power devices stays with OSAT because capex payback is long and switching costs are real.

## Cloudberry relevance

| Approach | Pitch floor | Value capture | Leading player |
|---|---|---|---|
| OSAT wire-bond | ~75 µm | Low | ASE |
| OSAT flip-chip / FOWLP | ~40 µm | Mid | Amkor |
| Foundry advanced pkg (CoWoS) | <10 µm | High | TSMC |


For Cloudberry, OSAT is infrastructure that portfolio companies must navigate, not invest in directly. The deal-flow angle: startups in photonics, compound semiconductors, or advanced materials often hit OSAT as a commercialisation bottleneck—packaging for III-V or SiPh dies is non-standard, and OSATs won't prioritise low-volume runs. Portfolio companies need OSAT partnerships or in-house back-end capability. GF's mature-node positioning maps cleanly onto OSAT partners serving automotive and RF—relevant for diligencing supply-chain risk in any GF-fabbed portfolio company.
