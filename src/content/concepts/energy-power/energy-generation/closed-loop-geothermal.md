# Closed-Loop Geothermal

*Kind: technology*

## Physics / mechanism

Closed-loop geothermal (CLG) — also called Advanced Geothermal Systems (AGS) or deep closed-loop — circulates a working fluid (water, CO₂, or supercritical fluid) through sealed wellbores drilled 3–10 km into hot dry rock. No hydraulic fracturing; no aquifer contact. Heat transfers conductively through the pipe wall from surrounding rock into the fluid, which returns to surface to drive ORC or steam turbines. Key parameters: rock thermal conductivity (~2–3 W/m·K for granite), temperature gradient (~25–35 °C/km typical, >50 °C/km in favourable basins), flow rate, and pipe surface area. Current systems (Eavor, Quaise targeting millimeter-wave drilling) demonstrate 5–20 MWth per well cluster; power density remains the binding constraint vs. open-loop hydrothermal. No seismic risk is the regulatory differentiator.

## Cloudberry relevance

Competes primarily with open-loop enhanced geothermal (EGS, e.g. Fervo, AltaRock) and conventional hydrothermal. Adjacent: deep borehole heat exchangers (shallow, <3 km, heating-only), co-produced geothermal from oil wells, and baseload alternatives (small modular reactors, long-duration storage + renewables).

| Approach | Seismic risk | Water use | Power density |
|---|---|---|---|
| CLG (closed-loop) | Negligible | None | Low (~1–5 W/m²) |
| EGS (open-loop fracture) | Moderate | Moderate | Medium (~5–15 W/m²) |
| Conventional hydrothermal | Low | High | High (site-dependent) |

---

CLG is not a direct Cloudberry portfolio target, but intersects via enabling hardware: high-temperature downhole sensors, SiC/GaN power electronics for ORC control, photonic distributed temperature sensing (DTS) along boreholes, and advanced ceramics/composites for drill strings. Quaise's millimeter-wave rock vaporisation (gyrotron-driven) is a plausible photonics adjacency. Watch for pick-and-shovel component suppliers rather than geothermal operators as the deal-flow angle.
